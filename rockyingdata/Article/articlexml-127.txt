            &lt;p&gt;
                Once upon a time, the fabric black was meant to be worn by housemaids in the mansions
                of America that made them look all covered up...
            &lt;/p&gt;
            &lt;img src="http://rockying.com/art/Lifestyle/Fashion/LBD/maids%201.jpg"
                title="Maids " alt="" style="width: 500px;" /&gt;
            &lt;p&gt;
                The white laces pronounced the curves of the sleeves and their waists but that was
                it. Calf-lengthed, short sleeved pieced with white aprons, this was oh-so-boring
                and old school!&lt;/p&gt;
            &lt;p&gt;
                This was prior to the 1920s where black symbolized only mourning.&lt;/p&gt;
        &lt;/div&gt;
        &lt;div class="slide"&gt;
            &lt;img src="http://rockying.com/art/Lifestyle/Fashion/LBD/Widows-in-Black.png"
                title="Widows in Black" alt="" style="width: 400px; height: 532px;" /&gt;
            &lt;h4&gt;
                The First World War changed the scenario as more and more women became widows and
                wearing black became “public”!
            &lt;/h4&gt;
        &lt;/div&gt;
        &lt;div class="slide"&gt;
            &lt;img src="http://rockying.com/art/Lifestyle/Fashion/LBD/Coco-chanel-LBD.png"
                title="Coco chanel" alt="" style="width: 400px; height: 540px;" /&gt;
            &lt;p&gt;
                Coco Chanel’s Little Black Dress changed the perception of the LBD completely! Women
                were swept off their feet to see Vogue’s cover picturing a woman in a cal-length-ed
                black dress which was a slash-neck, simple, finely cut and fitted. It was so simple
                and stunning that this changed the face of the Little Black dress for ever. What
                more? The LBD gets fascinatingly shorter and the woman gets to be the femme fatale.
                With this, Coco captured the tone of the times. Interesting to see how a garment
                changes history!&lt;/p&gt;
        &lt;/div&gt;
        &lt;div class="slide"&gt;
            &lt;img src="http://rockying.com/art/Lifestyle/Fashion/LBD/4th.slide.jpg"
                title="The LBD worn by a woman " alt="" style="width: 400px; height: 556px;" /&gt;
            &lt;p&gt;
                In the meantime, the black dress being worn publicly was criticized a lot and women
                who wore it in public gatherings were either treated as advocators of Puritanism,
                infamous and or mourning for the dead.&lt;/p&gt;
        &lt;/div&gt;
        &lt;div class="slide"&gt;
            &lt;img src="http://rockying.com/art/Lifestyle/Fashion/LBD/Annex%20-%20Hayworth,%20Rita%20%28Gilda%29_03.jpg"
                itle="Annex Hayworth" alt="" style="width: 400px; height: 488px;" /&gt;
            &lt;p&gt;
                Hollywood re-defined the chequered past of LBD forever. Black was now the color
                of high street fashionistas. Rita Hayworth’s &lt;a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gilda"
                    target="_blank"&gt;Gilda&lt;/a&gt; left the audience breathless and opened a new horizon
                as to what the LBD could do! Kan LBD with killer black satin gloves, America saw
                many divorces during the Gilda-phase.
            &lt;/p&gt;
        &lt;/div&gt;
        &lt;div class="slide"&gt;
            &lt;img src="http://rockying.com/art/Lifestyle/Fashion/LBD/Audrey%20Hepburn.jpg"
                title="Audrey Hepburn" alt="" style="width: 400px; height: 579px;" /&gt;
            &lt;p&gt;
                Audrey Hepburn continued to set the screen on fire with the LBD. The World War,
                America and men across the globe suffered catastrophes of the humble heart to see
                the women dressed in gorgeous black. Hepburn’s movie &lt;a target="_blank" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Breakfast_at_Tiffany%27s_%28film%29"&gt;
                    Breakfast at Tiffany’s&lt;/a&gt; saw the auction of the LBD at $800,000 beating Marilyn
                Monroe’s white dress! Anything for fashion, eh?
            &lt;/p&gt;
